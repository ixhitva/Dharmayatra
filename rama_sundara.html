Ramayana: Sundara Kanda

The Sundara Kanda, the fifth book of Valmiki’s Ramayana, is a radiant chapter centered on Hanuman’s heroic journey to Lanka in search of Sita. Named for its poetic beauty and Hanuman’s divine exploits, this Kanda showcases devotion, courage, and resilience. Hanuman’s leap across the ocean, his encounters in Ravana’s golden city, and his unwavering loyalty to Rama illuminate the path of dharma. Hanuman’s Journey to Lanka

Hanuman, emboldened by his devotion to Rama, stood at the edge of the ocean, his form swelling with divine strength. The vanaras watched in awe as he prepared to leap across the vast waters to Lanka, Ravana’s island fortress. With a mighty bound, Hanuman soared into the sky, his silhouette a beacon of hope against the rising sun. The ocean churned below, and celestial beings blessed his path. Yet, challenges arose: the demoness Surasa, testing his resolve, expanded her jaws to swallow him. Hanuman, with his quick wit, shrank to the size of a thumb, darted through her mouth, and emerged victorious. Further along, the mountain Mainaka rose from the sea, offering rest, but Hanuman, driven by duty, respectfully declined and pressed on.

Upon reaching Lanka, Hanuman faced the demoness Simhika, who sought to drag him into the depths. With a swift strike, he vanquished her, landing on Lanka’s shores under the cover of night. The city gleamed with golden palaces and towering walls, a testament to Ravana’s opulence and power. Hanuman, shrinking to the size of a cat to avoid detection, navigated the labyrinthine streets, evading guards and rakshasas. His heart burned with purpose as he searched for Sita, his every step guided by Rama’s ring, a token of love and trust carried close to his heart.

Lanka, with its grandeur, was a stark contrast to the forest’s simplicity, yet it was steeped in adharma. Hanuman’s journey, a blend of physical prowess and spiritual devotion, set the stage for his pivotal role in Rama’s quest, embodying the ideal of selfless service.

Finding Sita and the Ashoka Grove

In Lanka, Hanuman searched tirelessly, his small form slipping through Ravana’s palace, where opulence masked cruelty. He witnessed the demon king’s harem, filled with women enthralled by Ravana’s power, but Sita was nowhere among them. Despair crept in until Hanuman reached the Ashoka grove, a serene yet sorrowful garden where Sita sat captive under a simsupa tree. Her face, pale with grief yet radiant with resolve, stirred Hanuman’s heart. Guarded by fierce rakshasis who taunted her, Sita clung to her faith in Rama, unaware of Hanuman’s presence in the branches above.

Hanuman, cautious yet determined, sang Rama’s praises softly to gain Sita’s trust. Dropping Rama’s ring before her, he revealed himself as Rama’s devoted servant. Sita, overcome with emotion, wept at the sight of the ring, her hope rekindled. Hanuman offered to carry her back to Rama, but Sita, bound by dharma, refused, insisting that Rama himself rescue her to restore his honor. She entrusted Hanuman with her chudamani (hair ornament) as a token for Rama, a symbol of her unwavering love. Their meeting, charged with mutual respect, strengthened Hanuman’s resolve to aid Rama’s cause.

Before leaving, Hanuman sought to gauge Ravana’s strength. He wreaked havoc in the Ashoka grove, uprooting trees and defeating guards, proclaiming his allegiance to Rama. Captured by Ravana’s son Indrajit, Hanuman was brought before the demon king. Undaunted, he warned Ravana of Rama’s wrath and, when his tail was set ablaze, used the fire to torch Lanka’s palaces, a fiery message of defiance. Hanuman then leaped back across the ocean to Kishkindha, delivering Sita’s chudamani to Rama, igniting hope and setting the stage for the impending war.

The Sundara Kanda, with its focus on Hanuman’s devotion and Sita’s resilience, is a testament to the power of faith and the triumph of dharma over adversity, paving the way for the epic battle in the Yuddha Kanda.
