Mahabharata: Tapatyopakhyana

The divine love story of Tapati, daughter of the Sun, and King Samvarana unfolds through devotion, longing, and cosmic harmony. Their union, blessed by Surya and sage Vasishtha, brings both joy and trials to a kingdom in need. Explore the accordion below to uncover this tale of celestial beauty and earthly duty.

Tapatyopakhyana: The Story of Tapati

Long ago, in the celestial realms, the radiant Sun God, Vivaswat (Surya), had a daughter named Tapati, younger sister of the divine Savitri. Tapati’s beauty was beyond compare—flawless, serene, and luminous like the golden rays of dawn. Her conduct was pure, her penance unmatched, and there was none among gods, asuras, yakshas, or mortals who could rival her grace. Her father, proud yet anxious, searched in vain for a man worthy of such a daughter.

On Earth, there lived a mighty and virtuous king named Samvarana, a ruler of the Kuru dynasty. Endowed with beauty, valor, and deep spiritual devotion, he had been performing intense worship of the Sun with garlands, incense, fasts, and sacred offerings. Surya, pleased by his unwavering devotion, decided that this noble king would be the ideal match for Tapati.

One day, while hunting in the wild, King Samvarana lost his steed to hunger and exhaustion. Wandering alone through the mountain forests, he caught sight of a maiden whose brilliance stunned him into stillness. She stood alone, her eyes deep and dark like lotus petals, her golden form glowing amidst the trees. She was so ethereal that the king believed she must be the goddess Sri or a living beam of sunlight. His heart was instantly ensnared.

Moved by overwhelming desire, the king approached her and asked, “Who are you, O beautiful one, and why do you wander alone in these woods? You seem more divine than any being I have ever known. My heart is ablaze with longing—grant me your love!” But the maiden said nothing. Instead, she vanished before his eyes, leaving him lost and aching.

King Samvarana beholds the radiant Tapati in the forest, struck by her divine beauty.

Desperate, the king searched the forest in vain. At last, the maiden appeared again. Seeing him broken by love, she spoke softly, “O king, I am not my own mistress. I am Tapati, daughter of the Sun, under my father's care. If your feelings are true, win me from my father through prayer and righteousness. You have captured my heart too, but I cannot act without his consent.” With those words, she ascended into the heavens once more.

King Samvarana, consumed by longing, fasted and meditated on the mountain for twelve nights, calling upon the sage Vasishtha. The wise sage, sensing the cause of the king’s torment, approached Surya himself in the celestial realm. With respect and humility, Vasishtha asked for Tapati’s hand on the king’s behalf. Surya, pleased, agreed joyfully. “Samvarana is noble, Vasishtha is wise, and Tapati is virtuous—what better union could there be?” Tapati was then given to the sage to be united with her beloved.

When Vasishtha returned, Tapati descended like lightning from the skies. Samvarana, seeing her once more, was overcome with joy. With the Rishi’s blessings, the two were married with sacred rites on that very mountain. Entrusting his kingdom to Vasishtha, the king chose to remain in the forest with his bride, living a life of blissful seclusion for twelve years.

But while the king rejoiced, his kingdom suffered. For twelve years, not a drop of rain fell. The earth withered, the rivers dried, and the people starved. The capital became a ghost town, lifeless and hollow. Seeing the plight of the people, Vasishtha brought the king and queen back from their secluded joy. The moment they returned, the skies opened and the rains poured. Life returned, the fields flourished, and the people rejoiced.

Restored to his throne, Samvarana ruled with wisdom, and alongside his divine queen Tapati, he performed grand sacrifices, bringing prosperity and peace to his realm once more.
