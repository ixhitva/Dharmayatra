Ramayana: Uttara Kanda

The Uttara Kanda, the seventh and final book of Valmiki’s Ramayana, concludes the epic with Rama’s triumphant return to Ayodhya and his reign as a just king. This Kanda delves into the complexities of dharma, exploring the challenges of kingship, societal expectations, and personal sacrifice. It recounts the poignant events surrounding Sita’s exile, the birth of Lava and Kusha, and the epic’s bittersweet closure. Rama’s Return and Reign

After Ravana’s defeat, Rama, Sita, Lakshmana, and the vanara allies returned to Ayodhya in the Pushpaka Vimana, a celestial chariot. The city, radiant with joy, welcomed its rightful king after fourteen years of exile. Rama’s coronation was a grand affair, with sages, citizens, and celestial beings in attendance. Crowned alongside Sita, Rama ushered in an era of prosperity, known as Rama Rajya, where justice, dharma, and compassion governed the land. The vanaras, including Hanuman and Sugriva, were honored, and Vibhishana returned to rule Lanka justly.

Rama’s reign was marked by wisdom and fairness, as he resolved disputes and protected his people. However, whispers among the citizens questioned Sita’s purity after her captivity in Lanka, despite her Agni Pariksha. Bound by his duty as king to uphold societal trust, Rama faced a heart-wrenching decision. To preserve the kingdom’s harmony, he reluctantly sent Sita into exile, a choice that tore at his soul. Pregnant and alone, Sita found refuge in the hermitage of Sage Valmiki, where she gave birth to twin sons, Lava and Kusha, raised in the ways of dharma and valor.

Rama, unaware of his sons, continued to govern Ayodhya, his heart heavy with Sita’s absence. The Uttara Kanda reveals the complexities of kingship, where personal love is sacrificed for public duty, setting the stage for the poignant reunion to come.

The Reunion and Conclusion

Years later, Lava and Kusha, trained by Valmiki, became skilled warriors and poets, reciting the Ramayana as composed by the sage. During a grand Ashvamedha Yagna performed by Rama, the twins arrived in Ayodhya, unaware of their lineage, and sang the epic before the court. Their voices, echoing Rama’s own valor and sorrow, moved the king to tears. Recognizing their resemblance to Sita, Rama inquired about their origins. Valmiki revealed that Lava and Kusha were his sons, and Sita, summoned from exile, stood before Ayodhya once more.

To quell lingering doubts, Sita invoked Mother Earth to attest to her purity. The earth parted, and Sita, with serene dignity, descended into its embrace, reuniting with her divine mother. Rama, devastated yet bound by dharma, accepted her departure as a testament to her unblemished virtue. Lava and Kusha were embraced as Ayodhya’s heirs, their legacy continuing Rama’s lineage. The Kanda concludes with Rama’s later years, where, after a long and just reign, he ascended to his divine form as Vishnu, returning to Vaikuntha with Lakshmana and his devoted followers.

The Uttara Kanda, with its themes of sacrifice, duty, and divine fulfillment, offers a bittersweet closure to the Ramayana, reflecting on the eternal balance of dharma and the human heart.
