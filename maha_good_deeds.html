Mahabharata: Fruits of Good Deeds

In the aftermath of the Kurukshetra war, Yudhishthira seeks wisdom from Bhishma, who lies on his bed of arrows. Through timeless teachings on karma, Bhishma reveals the eternal rewards of good deeds and the path to spiritual liberation. Fruits of Good Deeds: A Conversation between Yudhishthira and Bhishma

As the Kurukshetra war ended and peace settled over the battlefield, Yudhishthira, weighed down by sorrow, sought wisdom. At the side of the fallen grandsire Bhishma, who lay upon his bed of arrows, he asked with a sincere heart, “O greatest of the Bharatas, revered and wise, tell me, what are the true fruits of good deeds? What awaits a man after death for the acts he commits in life?”

Bhishma, filled with calm and ancient knowledge, smiled faintly and said, “O Yudhishthira, listen well. What you ask is sacred knowledge, passed through the minds of Rishis and sages. The deeds performed by beings while alive, whether good or evil, do not vanish—they follow the soul, manifesting again in the same or similar forms. The actions of the body bear fruit in the waking world, while those of the mind show themselves in dreams. No act, however small, done with the senses or mind, is ever lost. The five sense organs, along with the eternal soul, stand witness to every action.”

He continued, “A man should offer hospitality with sincerity—offering food, water, kind words, shelter, and attention. This is the Panchadakshina sacrifice—the sacrifice of five gifts. One who feeds tired and unknown travelers gains great merit. Those who sleep on the ground by the sacred fire win comfort in future lives. Those who wear rags and bark today will find themselves adorned in fine garments tomorrow. One who turns away from worldly pleasures gains power and vehicles; one who abstains from meat earns children and wealth.”

Bhishma imparts timeless wisdom to Yudhishthira on the fruits of good deeds.

Bhishma’s voice, though faint, carried deep truth. He told how those who live a life of restraint, of celibacy, of stillness or meditation, gain the power to fulfill their deepest wishes. He described the warrior who dies with his face to the battlefield, and how such valor brings eternal rewards. “Charity,” he said, “leads to riches. Silence brings command. Austerity brings joy. Celibacy grants long life. And ahimsa, or non-injury, brings beauty and health.”

He spoke of those who lived on fruits, roots, or even air, who reached heavenly abodes for their sacrifice. He described the value of truth, the nobility gained through sacrifice, and how the soul climbs ever higher when freed from the hunger for pleasure. “Heaven belongs to the man of truth,” Bhishma said. “Great rewards come to the one who can master his desires, for desire does not die even when the body withers. Like a calf who finds its mother among a thousand cows, so too do our past actions find us across lifetimes. As fruit appears seasonally on a tree without urging, so too do deeds ripen in their own time.”

Yudhishthira listened, silent, as Bhishma explained how even the gods were pleased by those who pleased their parents and teachers. “Prajapati,” he said, “is honoured through devotion to the father; the Earth, through service to the mother; and Brahma, through respect for the teacher. These three—father, mother, and preceptor—are the very form of virtue. Those who neglect them see their good deeds turn to dust.”

As Bhishma ended, the princes of the Kuru race, gathered around him, were filled with awe and joy. His words had pierced their hearts, and a new light of understanding dawned upon them.

With the breath of wisdom still on his lips, Bhishma added one final lesson. “As mantras chanted without true devotion, or sacrifices done without proper gifts and hymns, lead to ruin, so too does falsehood in speech lead to evil. This is the ancient law of karma—the fruit of deeds, both good and bad. I have spoken it to you as the Rishis once spoke it. Tell me now, O son of Dharma, what more would you ask of me?”

And in that quiet moment, under the watch of the setting sun and the stillness of a field once soaked in blood, wisdom bloomed—gentle and eternal.
