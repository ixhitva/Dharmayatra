Mahabharata: Treachery at Kurukshetra

In the heart of the Kurukshetra War, the Mahabharata unveils a gripping tale of vengeance, deception, and the blurred lines of dharma. "Treachery at Kurukshetra" captures the tragic fall of young Abhimanyu, Arjuna’s fierce resolve, and Krishna’s strategic interventions to restore righteousness. This story explores the moral dilemmas of war, where both sides bend the rules, yet Krishna justifies such acts to uphold a greater purpose. Delve into the accordion below to uncover this intense narrative of sacrifice and strategy.

Treachery at Kurukshetra

In the Kurukshetra war, Abhimanyu, only 17, broke into the deadly Chakravyuha formation but didn’t know how to exit it. He fought heroically but was killed unfairly by multiple Kaurava warriors after losing his weapons—violating the laws of war. Enraged, Arjuna vowed to kill Jayadratha by sunset or take his own life. The Kauravas fiercely protected Jayadratha. Meanwhile, Arjuna’s ally Satyaki left Yudhishthira unguarded to fight Burishwara and was nearly killed. Krishna urged Arjuna to save Satyaki by breaking the war code. Arjuna reluctantly shot Burishwara from the side. Ashamed of his role in Abhimanyu’s death, Burishwara withdrew, but Satyaki, regaining consciousness, beheaded him in cold blood.

The fierce battlefield of Kurukshetra, where treachery and dharma collided.

As Arjuna struggled to reach Jayadratha in time, Krishna used an illusion to create a false sunset. Thinking the day was over, Jayadratha relaxed. Krishna then lifted the illusion, and Arjuna killed him. Later, when Karna was about to kill Arjuna with a divine weapon, Krishna sank Arjuna’s chariot slightly, saving him. The weapon missed, and Arjuna survived. Though these were acts of deception, Krishna justified them, saying: “Yes, we too are breaking dharma—but to restore it. The Kauravas use adharma to uphold evil. We use it to destroy evil.” In a war where the other side never played fair, Krishna believed the purpose—reestablishing righteousness—justified the means.
