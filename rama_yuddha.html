Ramayana: Yuddha Kanda

The Yuddha Kanda, the sixth book of Valmiki’s Ramayana, narrates the climactic war between Rama and Ravana, a battle of dharma against adharma. With Hanuman’s return bearing news of Sita, Rama mobilizes his vanara army, led by Sugriva, to cross the ocean and storm Lanka. The construction of the Rama Setu, fierce battles, and the ultimate confrontation with Ravana define this Kanda, showcasing courage, strategy, and divine intervention. The Rama Setu and March to Lanka

With Hanuman’s return and Sita’s chudamani in hand, Rama’s resolve hardened. He rallied the vanara army, a formidable force led by Sugriva, Hanuman, Angada, and Jambavan, to march toward the southern ocean. The challenge of crossing the vast waters loomed large. Advised by the ocean god Varuna, Rama meditated for three days, his patience tested. When the ocean remained unyielding, Rama’s divine wrath stirred, and he prepared to unleash his celestial bow. Moved by his power, Varuna relented, revealing that Nala, a skilled vanara architect, could construct a bridge—Rama Setu—to Lanka.

The vanaras, with boundless energy, gathered stones, trees, and boulders, inscribing Rama’s name on each. Guided by Nala’s expertise, they built the Rama Setu across the ocean, a marvel of engineering that spanned the waves. The bridge, shimmering under the sun, stood as a testament to unity and devotion. Rama, Lakshmana, and the vanara army crossed the Setu, their spirits high as they approached Lanka’s fortified shores. The city, with its golden ramparts, braced for war, as Ravana prepared his rakshasa legions, confident in his invincibility.

Rama’s army encamped on Lanka’s outskirts, their roars echoing through the hills. Vibhishana, Ravana’s righteous brother, defected to Rama’s side, unable to condone his brother’s adharma. Welcomed by Rama, Vibhishana provided crucial insights into Lanka’s defenses, strengthening their strategy. The stage was set for a war that would shake the heavens, as Rama’s forces prepared to breach Ravana’s stronghold to rescue Sita and restore dharma.

The War and Ravana’s Defeat

The war for Lanka erupted with ferocity, as Rama’s vanara army clashed with Ravana’s rakshasas. The battlefield, strewn with arrows and blood, resounded with the clash of celestial weapons. Hanuman, Angada, and Sugriva fought valiantly, toppling rakshasa warriors. Ravana’s son Indrajit, a master of illusion, unleashed the Nagapasha, binding Rama and Lakshmana in serpent coils. Garuda, Vishnu’s divine eagle, descended to free them, restoring their strength. Lakshmana faced Indrajit in a fierce duel, slaying him with a celestial arrow, a blow that shook Ravana’s confidence.

Ravana, enraged, entered the battlefield, his ten heads towering over the chaos. His chariot, drawn by monstrous beasts, thundered as he unleashed devastating weapons. Rama, wielding the Vaishnava bow, countered with divine precision, their duel a cosmic spectacle. The gods, watching from the heavens, sent Indra’s chariot to aid Rama. As the battle reached its climax, Rama invoked the Brahmastra, a weapon of ultimate destruction, guided by divine will. The arrow pierced Ravana’s heart, ending his reign of tyranny. The ten-headed king fell, his pride shattered, and Lanka mourned yet rejoiced in liberation.

With Ravana defeated, Rama crowned Vibhishana as Lanka’s king, restoring dharma to the land. Sita, freed from captivity, underwent the Agni Pariksha, a trial by fire, to prove her purity, emerging unscathed as the gods bore witness. Reunited, Rama and Sita, with Lakshmana and the vanara army, prepared to return to Ayodhya, their mission fulfilled. The Yuddha Kanda, with its epic battles and moral triumphs, underscores the victory of righteousness and the power of devotion.
