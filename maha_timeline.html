Mahabharata Timeline

Circa 3360 BCE
Shantanu Marries Ganga
King Shantanu of the Kuru dynasty marries the river goddess Ganga, who agrees to live as a mortal. Their union produces Devavrata, later known as Bhishma, who takes a formidable vow of celibacy and renunciation of the throne, earning his title and shaping the future of the Kuru lineage.
Circa 3355 BCE
Shantanu Marries Satyavati
After Ganga’s departure, Shantanu falls in love with Satyavati, a fisherwoman. Bhishma vows to never claim the throne or marry, ensuring Satyavati’s children inherit the kingdom. Their marriage results in the birth of Chitrangada and Vichitravirya, who both die young, leaving the Kuru lineage in crisis.
Circa 3350 BCE
Niyoga by Vyasa
To preserve the Kuru lineage after Vichitravirya’s death, Satyavati calls upon her son Vyasa, a sage, to father children through Niyoga with Vichitravirya’s widows, Ambika and Ambalika. This results in the birth of Dhritarashtra (blind), Pandu (pale), and Vidura (born to a maid), setting the stage for the future conflict between their descendants.
Circa 3345 BCE
Pandu’s Marriage
Pandu, chosen as king over his blind brother Dhritarashtra, marries Kunti and Madri. Kunti possesses a boon to invoke gods for children, while Madri brings her own grace to the union. Their marriages lay the foundation for the birth of the Pandavas, who will become central figures in the epic.
Circa 5600 BCE
Birth of Pandu and Dhritarashtra
King Vichitravirya dies without heirs. Satyavati calls upon Vyasa to father children with Vichitravirya’s widows, Ambika and Ambalika. Dhritarashtra, born blind, and Pandu, born pale, are raised as princes of Hastinapura, setting the stage for the Kuru dynasty’s succession disputes.
Circa 5580 BCE
Birth of the Pandavas and Kauravas
Pandu, cursed to die if he engages in intimacy, retires to the forest. Kunti uses a boon to invoke gods, giving birth to Yudhishthira, Bhima, and Arjuna. Madri bears Nakula and Sahadeva. Meanwhile, Dhritarashtra’s wife Gandhari gives birth to 100 sons, the Kauravas, led by Duryodhana.
Circa 5570 BCE
Pandu’s Death
Pandu, overcome by desire, dies after embracing Madri, who immolates herself. Kunti returns to Hastinapura with the five Pandavas. The Pandavas and Kauravas grow up together under Bhishma’s care, but tensions arise due to Duryodhana’s jealousy and ambition.
Circa 3335–3320 BCE
Training Under Kripacharya
The Pandavas and Kauravas grow up under the tutelage of Kripacharya, the royal preceptor of the Kuru dynasty. He imparts foundational skills in warfare, Vedic knowledge, and statecraft, preparing the young princes for their roles. This early education fosters initial camaraderie but also sows seeds of rivalry, as the cousins’ ambitions and talents begin to diverge.
Circa 3325 BCE
Karna Challenges Arjuna
Karna, a skilled warrior of mysterious origin, appears at a public display of arms and challenges Arjuna in archery. Mocked for his supposed low birth, Karna is befriended by Duryodhana, who crowns him king of Anga. This event marks the beginning of Karna’s lifelong rivalry with Arjuna and his alliance with the Kauravas.
Circa 5565 BCE
Training Under Drona
The Pandavas and Kauravas are trained by Guru Drona in warfare and archery. Arjuna excels, earning Drona’s favor and the divine bow Gandiva. Drona demands Ekalavya’s thumb as guru-dakshina to ensure Arjuna’s supremacy, highlighting the intense rivalry among the cousins.
Circa 3315 BCE
Ekalavya’s Sacrifice
Ekalavya, a tribal prince and self-taught archer, surpasses even Arjuna in skill. When Drona discovers Ekalavya learned indirectly from him, he demands Ekalavya’s thumb as guru-dakshina to protect Arjuna’s status. Ekalavya complies, sacrificing his archery prowess, highlighting Drona’s bias and the tragic cost of loyalty.
Circa 3310 BCE
Lakshagraha Conspiracy
Duryodhana, fueled by jealousy, conspires with Shakuni to kill the Pandavas by inviting them to a festival in Varnavat and housing them in a palace made of lac, a flammable substance. Warned by Vidura, the Pandavas escape through a secret tunnel, while the world believes they perished in the fire, forcing them into hiding in the forest.
Circa 5563 BCE
Draupadi’s Swayamvara
Arjuna wins Draupadi’s hand at her swayamvara by hitting a fish’s eye with an arrow. The Pandavas share Draupadi as their wife due to Kunti’s command. This event angers the Kauravas, particularly Duryodhana, escalating tensions between the two families.
Circa 3300 BCE
Founding of Indraprastha
The Pandavas return to Hastinapura, revealing they are alive. To avoid conflict, Dhritarashtra grants them the barren land of Khandavaprastha. With Krishna’s guidance and the help of the demon architect Maya, the Pandavas transform it into the magnificent city of Indraprastha, establishing their own kingdom.
Circa 3300 BCE
Construction of Mayasabha
Maya, the demon architect, builds the magical palace Mayasabha for the Pandavas in Indraprastha. Its illusory floors and walls dazzle visitors, but Duryodhana’s humiliation during a visit—slipping on the deceptive floors—fuels his envy and desire for revenge against the Pandavas.
Circa 5562 BCE
Game of Dice
Duryodhana, with Shakuni’s help, invites Yudhishthira to a rigged dice game. Yudhishthira loses his kingdom, brothers, and Draupadi. Draupadi’s humiliation in the Kaurava court, saved by Krishna’s intervention, fuels the Pandavas’ resolve for revenge.
Circa 5562–5549 BCE
Pandavas’ Exile
The Pandavas, with Draupadi, are exiled for 13 years (12 years in the forest and 1 year incognito). They face hardships, gain divine weapons, and form alliances with kings like Drupada and Virata, preparing for the inevitable war against the Kauravas.
Circa 3283 BCE
Virata Parva
In their final year of exile, the Pandavas live incognito in the Matsya kingdom under King Virata. Arjuna disguises himself as Brihannala, a dance teacher. When the Kauravas attack Matsya, Arjuna reveals himself, defeating them with his divine weapons, signaling the end of their exile and the approach of war.
Circa 3282 BCE
Failed Peace Negotiation
After completing their exile, the Pandavas demand Indraprastha back. Krishna, acting as their envoy, proposes peace, asking for just five villages. Duryodhana’s refusal to cede even a needlepoint of land makes war inevitable. Both sides form alliances, with Krishna choosing to guide Arjuna as his charioteer.
16 Oct 5561 BCE
War Begins; Bhagavad Gita
The Kurukshetra War commences. Bhishma leads the Kauravas, causing heavy Pandava losses. Arjuna hesitates to fight, leading to Krishna’s recitation of the Bhagavad Gita, imparting spiritual wisdom and urging Arjuna to fulfill his Kshatriya duty.
25 Oct 5561 BCE
Bhishma Falls
Shikhandi, a reincarnation of Amba, faces Bhishma, who lowers his weapons due to his vow. Arjuna shoots countless arrows, felling Bhishma, who lies on a bed of arrows. The Kauravas are demoralized, and Drona takes command.
28 Oct 5561 BCE
Abhimanyu’s Death
Drona forms the Chakravyuha. Abhimanyu enters but is trapped and killed by multiple Kaurava warriors, including Drona, Karna, and Dushasana’s son, after Jayadratha blocks the Pandavas from aiding him. Arjuna vows revenge.
29 Oct 5561 BCE
Jayadratha Falls
Arjuna, enraged by Abhimanyu’s death, vows to kill Jayadratha before sunset. Krishna creates an illusion of sunset, allowing Arjuna to behead Jayadratha. The battle continues into the night, with Ghatotkacha killed by Karna’s Shakti weapon.
30 Oct 5561 BCE
Drona’s Death
Yudhishthira announces the death of an elephant named Ashwatthama, leading Drona to believe his son is dead. Drona drops his weapons, and Dhrishtadyumna beheads him, fulfilling his destiny as Drona’s slayer.
1 Nov 5561 BCE
Karna’s Death
Arjuna and Karna engage in a climactic duel. Karna’s chariot wheel gets stuck due to a curse. Urged by Krishna, Arjuna shoots Karna, killing him. The Kauravas are further demoralized, and Shalya becomes the new commander.
2 Nov 5561 BCE
Duryodhana’s Defeat; War Ends
Duryodhana hides in a lake but is found by the Pandavas. Bhima defeats him in a mace fight, breaking his thigh. Ashwatthama attacks the Pandava camp at night, killing Draupadi’s sons. The war ends with the Pandavas’ victory.
Circa 5561 BCE
Yudhishthira’s Coronation
Yudhishthira is crowned king of Hastinapura. The Pandavas restore dharma, but the war’s immense loss leaves them reflective. Krishna advises them on governance, and the Mahabharata’s teachings continue through the ages.

Post-Mahabharata Events

Circa 5561–5525 BCE
Yudhishthira’s Rule
Yudhishthira rules Hastinapura for 36 years, guided by Krishna’s counsel. He establishes dharma, overseeing a period of recovery and governance after the devastating Kurukshetra War.
Circa 5525 BCE
Krishna’s Departure
Krishna is accidentally shot by a hunter at Prabhas, marking his departure from the mortal world. The Yadava dynasty collapses due to internal conflicts, fulfilling a curse.
Circa 5525 BCE
Pandavas’ Final Journey
The Pandavas and Draupadi undertake their Mahaprasthana, a great journey to the Himalayas. They ascend to heaven, leaving behind their earthly responsibilities.
Circa 5525 BCE
Parikshit’s Coronation
Abhimanyu’s son Parikshit is crowned king of Hastinapura, continuing the Kuru lineage after the Pandavas’ departure.
Circa 5500 BCE
Janamejaya’s Snake Sacrifice
Janamejaya, Parikshit’s son, performs the Sarpa Satra to avenge his father’s death by snakebite. Vaishampayana narrates the Mahabharata during this sacrifice, preserving its legacy.
